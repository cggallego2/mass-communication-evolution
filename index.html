  1. Origin of Speech

    100000 BCE

    The origin of speech marked the beginning of human communication.

  2. Development of Symbols

    30000 BCE

    The imperfection of language allowed ideas to spread more easily, eventually leading to the development of new forms of communication that improved both the range with which people could communicate and the longevity of information. All of these inventions were based on the fundamental concept of the symbol.

    Gestures, signs, objects, signals, and words are examples of symbols that help people understand the world around them. They provide clues to understanding experiences by conveying recognizable meanings that are shared by societies.

    Individuals who are unable or have difficulty communicating using speech alone frequently use symbol-based communication. Symbols provide a visual representation of a word or idea.

    The discovery of such alleviated the limitations of pure oral communication. People now have the ability to share ideas that cannot be described by voice. For example, if one discovers a strange animal that no one else has seen, using symbols to describe it will undoubtedly be useful.

  7. First Gutenburg Bible Printed

    1456

    The Gutenberg Bible was one of the first books printed using Johannes Gutenberg's printing press. The print quality was so high that the future Pope Pius II told Cardinal Carvajal that the Bibles are "exceedingly clean and correct in their script, and without error, such that Your Excellency could read effortlessly without glasses." According to historians, approximately 180 copies were printed, with only 49 remaining today.

  8. First Long-Distance Telegraph Line

    1792

    This system delivered messages over long distances much faster than postmen and, once built, had lower long-term operating costs. It is a series of stations, usually towers, whose purpose is to transmit textual information via visual cues. The first successful optical telegraph was built in 1792 by French engineer Claude Chappe and his brothers, who covered France with a network of 556 stations spanning 4,800 kilometers. Until the 1850s, the Chappe system was used for military and national communications.

    1884

    Nipkow's invention of a rotating disk (Nipkow disk) with one or more spirals of apertures that passed successively across the picture in 1884 enabled the development of a mechanical television system. Electronic scanning devices supplanted the Nipkow disk in 1934.

  17. First Broadcast of a Radio Station

    1920

    KDKA, the first commercial radio station, went on air for the first time on November 2, 1920. It aired with the results from the Harding-Cox presidential election before it was published in any newspaper at that time.

  18. Invention of Mechanical Television

    1925

    John Logie Baird publicly demonstrated the first working mechanical television on January 26,1926. This version of mechanical television has a scan rate of 5 pictures per second and shows the potential of televisions.

  19. Invention of Electronic Television

    1927

    At the age of 15, Philo Farnsworth invented the world's first all-electric television. By the time he died, he had over 300 U.S. and foreign patents for electronic and mechanical devices.

  20. Commercialization of Electronic Televisions

    1934

    Telefunken in Germany produced the first commercially available electronic televisions with cathode ray tubes in 1934.

  21. First Television Advertisement

    1941

    The world's first television commercial aired on July 1, 1941, during a baseball game between the Brooklyn Dodgers and the Philadelphia Phillies. The advertisement was for Bulova Watches, which is still in business today. It lasted only 10 seconds and aired on WNBT, a local New York channel.

  22. Creation of Videotape Recorder

    1951

    Charles Ginsburg led the Ampex Corporation research team that created the first practical videotape recorder (VTR). A rapidly rotating recording head was used to apply high-frequency signals to a reel of magnetic tape in the system. The videotape recorder (VTR) revolutionized television broadcasting.

  23. Invention of Long Distance Xerography

    1964

    Xerox Corporation introduced LDX (Long Distance Xerography) in 1964, which is regarded as the first "commercial" version of today's fax machine. Xerox's Magnafax Telecopier, introduced in 1966, could be connected to any telephone line and could send a letter-sized document in six minutes.

  24. Invention of Cellular Phone

    1973

    Martin Cooper invented the first handheld cellular mobile phone (distinct from the car phone) in 1973 while working at Motorola, and led the team that developed it and brought it to market in 1983.

  26. Invention of World Wide Web

    1989

    Tim Berners-Lee, a British scientist, created the World Wide Web (WWW) while working at CERN in 1989. The web was created to meet the demand for automated information sharing among scientists in universities and institutes around the world.

  27. First SMS Was Sent

    1992

    Neil Papworth, a 22-year-old software programmer from the United Kingdom, sent the first text message from a computer to his colleague Richard Jarvis in 1992. Neil had been working as a developer and test engineer for his client, Vodafone, to create a Short Message Service (SMS). The very first text message, sent on December 3, 1992, simply said, "Merry Christmas."

  28. Development of Social Media

    1997

    Andrew Weinreich founded SixDegrees, a social network that predated Facebook by approximately 7 years, in 1996. SixDegrees was reportedly sold for $125 million in 1999. He went on to found several businesses, including MeetMoi, a location-based dating site that he sold to Match.com's parent company.

  29. First Mass Social Networking Site

    2001

    Due to Korea's high Internet penetration rate, the first mass social networking site was the South Korean service, Cyworld, which launched as a blog-based site in 1999 and added social networking features in 2001. It was also one of the first companies to profit from the sale of virtual goods.
